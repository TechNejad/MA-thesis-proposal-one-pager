Playing the Part
How Role Specifications Shape LLM Agent Interactions
Mohsen Hassan Nejad | Master’s in Artificial Intelligence for Sustainable Societies (AISS), 2025–2026
Master’s thesis proposal
Core Idea
This thesis investigates how Role Specifications—structured framings that bundle an LLM agent’s persona, goals, norms, tactics, time beliefs and contextual pressures—shape interactions in mixed‑motive resource sharing. It explores role‑based framing as a soft‑alignment mechanism to foster cooperation, trust and adaptability among agents.
Literature Gap
Existing work uses role prompts or contextual personas (e.g., “buyer”, “referee”) yet treats them as scaffolding; no unified schema defines what a role comprises or how much control it imposes. This thesis makes Role Specifications the explicit object of study.
Main Research Question
How do Role Specifications influence cooperation, communication and adaptation dynamics among LLM agents in repeated social dilemmas, and where do they fall short?
Simulation Setup
  • Two agents representing neighbouring cities negotiate monthly water allocation during a drought, choosing to cooperate (fair split) or defect (over‑draw); a pre‑decision negotiation phase allows persuasion and deception.
  • Roles tested: Sustainability Officer (norm‑enforcing, long‑term), City Growth Planner (demand‑driven, politically pressured), Water Security Commissioner (risk‑averse, graduated sanctions).
  • Each role bundles goals, norms, tactics, time beliefs, red lines, disclosure policies and private pressures; the design draws on Ostrom’s commons‑governance principles and Axelrod’s iterated‑game strategies.
Data Collection & Analysis
  • Full negotiation transcripts, tagged events (persuasion, defection, role adherence/drift), cooperation outcomes over rounds and post‑simulation “agent interviews”.
  • Comparative analysis across role pairings via Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) to identify conditions for trust, conflict and cooperation.
Theoretical Foundations
  • Game theory: IPD dynamics, shadow of the future.
  • Dialogue theory: commitments, persuasion tactics, cheap talk.
  • Symbolic interactionism: roles as performed, not fixed.
  • Commons governance (Ostrom) & evolutionary strategies (Axelrod).
  • AI alignment: role‑prompting as a soft‑control strategy.
Expected Contribution & Deliverables
  • Introduce Role Specifications as a unified variable in multi‑agent LLM research.
  • Provide empirical evidence on how role framing affects cooperation, persuasion and adaptation under uncertainty.
  • Offer practical guidance for designing prosocial, interpretable and adaptable agent societies in resource‑sharing settings.
  • Deliverables include a thesis paper, open‑source simulation code, policy briefs, blog posts, academic posters, slide deck, role‑spec templates, and a dataset with transcripts.
