Towards an Ontology of Normative Role Design for Multi-Agent LLM Systems
Work-in-progress master's thesis
The Central Problem
Large language models are evolving into autonomous agents increasingly deployed in multi-agent systems. A key mechanism for designing such agents is the normative roles encoded into their system prompts, which define behavioral expectations. However, the development and evaluation of these roles remain ad hoc and unsystematic, guided by trial-and-error rather than coherent principles. The field lacks a shared vocabulary and standardized methods, making it difficult to evaluate how norms are encoded and what effects they produce.
Objective
To develop a preliminary ontology that maps and categorizes how normative roles are designed, implemented, and operationalized within multi-agent LLM simulations.
Why it Matters
  1. AI agents are being deployed in critical domains: From energy grids and supply chains to healthcare and education.
  2. Design choices encode norms: How we prompt and structure agents shapes their decision-making and societal impact.
  3. Scale amplifies risk: These systems operate at massive scale, affecting millions of users and real-world outcomes.
Main Research Question
What types of normative roles are operationalized across different applications of multi-agent systems?
Research Approach
Approach: Constructing a preliminary ontology using systematic literature review of multi-agent simulation research papers and experiments.
Data: Multi-agent LLM simulation papers and their GitHub repositories.
Units of Analysis:
  1. System Prompts (core object) — Natural language role instructions embedded in model's code base
  2. Simulation Papers — Contextual data: simulation domain, architecture, evaluation, outcomes
Analytical Process:
1. Systematic Literature Review – Search, identify and select multi-agent LLM simulation studies as the foundation for shaping the ontology
2. Five-Layer Coding – Code each study across: (a) Study Context, (b) Simulation setup, (c) Normative Design (role types, methods, ethics), (d) Outcomes (behaviours, metrics), (e) Evidence (prompts, contradictions)
3. Ontology Construction – Formalize findings as knowledge triples (subject-relation-object) to map role types, prompting methods, ethical framings, and their relationships to outcomes
Theoretical Framework
Three normative ethical theories guide the analysis:
Deontological
rules, duties, obligations
Consequentialist
outcomes, utility, optimization
Virtue Ethics
character traits, social roles
Based on Woodgate & Ajmeri (2024), Macro Ethics Principles for Responsible AI Systems
Expected Contribution
Shared vocabulary for a fragmented field · Mapping of role types, prompting methods, and outcomes · Detection of design contradictions and failure modes · Foundation for systematic evaluation and governance
Deliverables: Thesis · Ontology · Conference papers · Briefs
