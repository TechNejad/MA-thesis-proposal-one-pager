Playing the Part
How Role Specifications Shape LLM Agent Interactions
Work‑in‑progress thesis proposal
Core Idea
This thesis investigates how Role Specifications—structured framings of an LLM agent’s persona, goals, norms, tactics, time belief and contextual pressures—shape agent interactions in a mixed-motive resource sharing setting. The project explores role‑based framing as a possible soft‑alignment mechanism for fostering cooperation, trust, and adaptability among LLM agents.
Literature Gap
Existing studies regularly use role prompts, personas, or contextual framings (e.g., “desperate,” “cunning,” “buyer,” “referee”) to get models to negotiate, coordinate, or reason, but treat these as background scaffolding rather than as a unified independent variable. There is no common design schema for what a “role” comprises or how much control it should impose. This thesis explicitly makes Role Specifications the object of study.
Main Research Question
How do Role Specifications influence cooperation, communication, and adaptation dynamics among LLM agents in repeated social dilemmas, and where do they fall short?
Simulation Setup
  • A resource sharing social dilemma modelled after the Iterated Prisoner’s Dilemma (IPD).
  • Two agents, representing neighbouring cities, negotiate monthly water allocations during a drought, choosing to cooperate (fair split) or defect (over‑draw). A pre‑decision negotiation phase allows persuasion and deception.
  • Summary of role specifications tested:
    • Sustainability Officer — norm‑enforcement, long‑termism.
    • City Growth Planner — demand‑driven, politically pressured short‑termism.
    • Water Security Commissioner — risk‑averse, graduated sanctions.
  • Each role bundles: goals, norms, tactics, time beliefs (a “shadow of the future”), red lines, disclosure policies, and private pressures.
  • Design lineage: role components are inspired by Ostrom’s commons‑governance principles (monitoring, reciprocity, fit to local needs, graduated sanctions) and Axelrod’s strategy archetypes from iterated games (niceness, forgiveness, retaliation, clarity).
Data Collection & Analysis
  • Full negotiation transcripts.
  • Tagged events: persuasion, defection, role adherence/drift.
  • Payoff metrics: cooperation rates and negotiation outcomes over rounds.
  • Post‑simulation “agent interviews” to probe reasoning.
  • Comparative analysis across role pairings using Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) to identify conditions for trust, conflict, and cooperation.
Theoretical Foundations
  • Game theory — IPD, payoff structures, shadow of the future.
  • Dialogue theory — commitments, persuasion tactics, cheap talk.
  • Symbolic interactionism — roles as performed, not fixed (role adherence vs. role drift).
  • Commons governance (Ostrom) and evolutionary strategy insights (Axelrod) as design priors for role modules and context.
  • AI alignment — role‑prompting as a soft‑control strategy.
Expected Contribution & Deliverables
  • Introduce Role Specifications as a unified, testable independent variable in multi‑agent LLM research.
  • Provide empirical evidence on how role framing affects cooperation, persuasion, and adaptation under uncertainty.
  • Offer practical guidance for designing prosocial, interpretable, and adaptable agent societies in resource‑sharing settings.
  • Deliverables include: thesis paper, open‑source simulation code, policy briefs, blog/LinkedIn posts, academic posters, slide deck, role‑spec templates, and a dataset with transcripts.
