Playing the Part
How Role Specifications Shape LLM Agent Interactions
Work‑in‑progress Thesis Proposal
Core Idea
This thesis investigates how structured Role Specifications—structured framing of an LLM agent’s persona, goals, norms, tactics and contextual pressures—shape agent interactions in multi‑agent simulations. The project explores role‑play as a soft alignment mechanism for fostering cooperation, trust and adaptability among language model agents.
Research Question
How do Role Specifications influence cooperation, communication and adaptation dynamics among LLM agents in repeated social dilemmas, and where do they fall short?
Simulation Setup
  • A resource‑sharing social dilemma modelled after the Iterated Prisoner’s Dilemma.
  • Two city agents (representatives of “City A” and “City B”) negotiate monthly water allocations during a drought. They can choose to cooperate (fair split) or defect (take more water), with an added pre‑commit negotiation phase for dynamic interactions.
  • Agents are assigned role specs such as:
    • Sustainability Officer — norm‑enforcer, long‑term planner.
    • City Growth Planner — short‑term, politically pressured.
    • Security Commissioner — risk‑averse, favours early protection.
  • Each role includes varied elements: goals, red lines, time beliefs (a “shadow of the future”), disclosure policies and rhetorical tactics.
Data Collection & Analysis
  • Transcripts of negotiation rounds.
  • Tagged events: persuasion, defection, role adherence and drift.
  • Payoff metrics: cooperation rates and negotiation outcomes.
  • Post‑simulation “agent interviews” to probe reasoning.
  • Use of Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) to explore conditions for trust, conflict and cooperation across role pairings.
Theoretical Foundations
  • Game Theory — IPD dynamics and payoff structures.
  • Dialogue Theory — commitments and persuasion tactics.
  • Symbolic Interactionism — roles as performed rather than fixed (Mead, Goffman).
  • Commons Governance — Ostrom’s design principles adapted for AI agents.
  • AI Alignment — role‑prompting as a soft‑control strategy.
Expected Contribution
  • Introduces Role Specifications as a novel experimental variable in multi‑agent LLM research.
  • Provides empirical insight into how behavioural framing affects cooperation and role adherence.
  • Informs the design of prosocial, interpretable and adaptable AI agents in decentralised ecosystems.
