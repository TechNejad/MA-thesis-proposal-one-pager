Performing Alignment
Exploring Role Specifications as Soft Control Mechanisms in a Resource-Sharing Simulation with LLM Agents
Research Context
LLM agents are entering socio-technical systems where coordination and resource management create systemic risks beyond single-model failures. Most studies treat roles as background setup, yet they fundamentally shape agent behavior. This research investigates whether structured Role Specifications—encompassing persona, goals, norms, tactics, time beliefs, and pressures—can reliably guide cooperation in mixed-motive settings.
How do Role Specifications influence cooperation, communication, and adaptation dynamics among LLM agents in repeated social dilemmas, and where do they fall short?
Methodology
Two LLM agents representing neighboring cities negotiate monthly water allocation during a drought. The simulation follows an Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma structure with pre-decision negotiation, uncertain duration (5-15 rounds), and structured role specifications as the independent variable.
Role Components
Persona, goals, norms, tactics, time beliefs, red lines, disclosure policies, private pressures
Data Collection
Negotiation transcripts, decision logs, payoff outcomes, post-simulation interviews
Agent Roles
  • Sustainability Officer: Long-term cooperation focus, norm enforcement, mutual monitoring
  • City Growth Planner: Demand-driven, flexible, politically pressured toward short-term gains
  • Water Security Commissioner: Risk-averse, favors early protection and graduated sanctions
Theoretical Framework
The work bridges game theory (Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, shadow of the future), symbolic interactionism (Goffman's role performance), Ostrom's commons governance principles, and contemporary research on LLM multi-agent systems. Role Specifications are examined as both descriptive tools and prescriptive levers for alignment.
Expected Contributions
  • Systematic evaluation of role specifications as soft-control mechanisms
  • Empirical insights on role adherence, adaptation, and drift under pressure
  • Practical design guidance for multi-agent cooperation in resource dilemmas
